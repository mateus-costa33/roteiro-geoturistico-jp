Vegetation

We cannot dissociate the plant formations from the abiotic elements of the area, since the sum of the climate, relief, hydrography and soil were essential to create the extremely biodiverse landscape mosaic in America of the South (MEDEIROS, 2009). Associated with these aspects, the figure of the human being appears, as a disintegrating and modifying element of nature, responsible for the destruction and, in some cases, practically extinction, of part of this flora, mainly from the 16th century.

The history of the Captaincy of Parahyba, portrayed in chapter 6, shows an economic evolution incompatible with the conservation of natural resources, notably plant formations, reflecting what happened on the scale of Brazilian territory. All economic cycles have degraded a large percentage of native vegetation, mainly the Atlantic Forest, which stretched from the Brazilian coast from Rio Grande do Norte to Rio Grande do Sul. Today, only about 4% of this natural cover remains (ALMEIDA, 2000a) and in Paraíba, the situation is no different.

The following classification was based on Paraíba (2004b), with the addition of some formations that were not included in this proposal, with plant formation divided into two classes: herbaceous (campestre) and arbustiva-arbórea (forestry). In the work area, remnants of forests have been transformed into Protection Areas, such as the 'Jardim Botânico Benjamin Maranhão' and the 'Restinga de Cabedelo National Forest', which will be highlighted in this chapter, as they have recreational and tourism potential, in addition to serve as important environmental indicators.

Characterization of plant formations

One of the most common vegetation formations in the area, where herbaceous and shrub elements mix with small trees are the savannah and savannah fields. Since this formation is located on the top of the boards, in the highest regions, it tends to be locally known as “Mata de Tabuleiro” or, simply, “Tabuleiro”. The cerrados are made up of two strata: a shrub-tree, forming a seasonal semi-deciduous forest, sparse among the herbaceous stratum, with xerophytic characteristics, with hard and rough leaves, small size, trunks and twisted branches, thick and cracked cortex and thick bark, protected by a layer of cork. The main species are mangabeiras ( Hancornia speciosa Gomes), bomeliáceas ( Achmea sp. ), broad-leaved muricis ( Byrsonima verbascifolia ) , trash can ( Curatella americana ), sapucaia ( Lecythis pisonis ), cajuí ( Anacardium microcarpum ), among others; an herbaceous stratum, located in lower lands and subject to a seasonal hydroperiod, called ‘Brejos’ but which, however, do not suffer marine influence, as in part of the holocenic marine terraces, which form the Restinga de Cabedelo. They occur through tufts that discontinuously cover the ground, with a height that does not exceed half a meter. The main species of grasses are cyperaceae and blechnaceous, such as those of the genera Echinolaena, Eragrostis and Aanicum , among others (CARVALHO and CARVALHO, 1985; PARAÍBA, 2004b). The main types of soils are the Spodosols. Commonly, they develop at the rear of the high water limit, when the salinity is lower and the presence of organic matter increases, together with a denser, more biodiverse and larger vegetation called Mata de Restinga, which will be described later.

Along the entire coastline of the study area, especially preserved on Intermares beach, in Cabedelo, on Tambaú / Cabo Branco beach, in João Pessoa and in much of the south coast, the presence of pioneer vegetation is common. beach , typical pantropical vegetation, located above the limits of high tides. From the cliff of Cabo Branco towards the south coast, this formation corresponds to the curb line that separates the post-beach from the holocenic marine terraces, being an indicator of coastal stability, as it is sensitive to marine abrasion. Thus, it corresponds to a herbaceous psamophilous vegetation, with halophilic characteristics and thick leaves, being associated with a Quartzene Neossol. The most common species of grasses are the beach parsley ( Ipomoea pescaprae ), guajeru ( Chvsobalanus icaco ), rattlesnake ( Crotalaria retusa ), silk cotton ( Calotropis procera ), beach grass ( Sporoblus virginicus ) and the beach bredo ( Blutaparon portulacoides ). The distance from the coast, with a decrease in salinity and an increase in organic matter, makes it possible for more frequent shrub species to flourish, such as the pine-tree ( Mariscus maritimus ) and ginger grass ( Cymbopogon martinii ), among others (ROCHA, 1996), as well as fruit trees such as cashew ( Anacardium occidentale L. ) and mangabeira ( Harconia speciosa ), in addition to coconut trees ( Cocos nucifera L. ).

The restinga fields correspond to pioneer restinga vegetation, with variable density, the most common species of which are beach murici ( Byrsonima gardneriana Juss ), pigeon eye ( Avrus precatorius l. ) and paquevira ( Heliconia angustifólia Hook ). They are being threatened by housing developments and agriculture, especially coconut plantations. The pioneer cliff vegetation occurs in the steep slopes of the inactive cliffs or in colluvial deposits at the bases, being herbaceous and shrub species that occur on the Plio-pleistocene sand-clay sediments of the Barreiras Formation . They are important indicators of intense and recent erosive inactivity and are, therefore, a fundamental point to differentiate inactive from active cliffs (FURRIER, 2007).

The hydrophilic, hygrophilic and halophilic fields are located on the river plains, in depressions close to the coast or bordering the mangroves, that is, in flat areas. What differentiates each of these formations depends on the water saturation level of the terrain. The hydrophilic fields are thus associated with the river plains and depressions close to the coast whose Gleisol is saturated in water, whether superficial or subsurface, as the water table is very close to the surface (DOMINGUEZ et al. , nineteen ninety). They correspond to herbaceous vegetation on the water levels, the most common species being paste ( Pistia sp. ), water hyacinth or baroness ( Eichhornia sp.), Nimphia sp., Pontederia sp. and Panicum aquaticum (ROCHA, 1996). The hygrophilic fields are located, geographically, in the same areas as the hydrophilic fields; but with sporadic flooding, whose soils are of the Neossolo Flúvico type and the predominant species are the Christmas grass ( Eriolaena rosea ), the reed ( Cyperus giganteus ), the board ( Typa dominguenses ) and silk grass ( Cynodon dactiolon ), according to Neves (2003). halophilic fields appear in the paludious depressions, which receive the ebb and flow of the tides, with the same indiscriminate mangrove soil but more consistent and firm. The main species found are the cardeiro ( Ceres triangularis ) and the côroa-de-frade ( Melocactus violaceus sensu PARAÍBA, 2004b; FURRIER, 2007).

As an arboreal and shrubby vegetation, the Atlantic Forest is the generic name for a variety of tropical humid forests that occur in the coastal regions of Brazil, following the humidity brought by the south-east trade winds, such as, for example, the ombrophilous lowland or open forest, when associated with river channels and at the top of the coastal boards; or humid hillside forest, especially in Serra do Mar, in the Southeast (ANDRADE LIMA and ROCHA, 1971), which is home to one of the highest degrees of species richness and endemism rates on the planet. At the time of the Discovery, this vegetal formation extended over an area of ​​1.3 million km2, of which there are currently no more than 4% of primary remnants in the medium stage of regeneration (BRASIL, 2004). Corresponds to a vegetation cover with an essentially arboreal stratum, with trees between 25 to 30 m in height, wide crowns, thick trunks, dense, of great biodiversity, with the presence of epiphytes, perennials, broadleaved, hygrophilic and, locally, with a stratum relatively dense and herbaceous shrub. In the trays this vegetation practically disappeared, with the advance of urbanization, leaving some spots around some courses of rivers, in the so-called 'grotões'. The bulandi ( Symphonia globulifera ) stands out in these areas.

In the study area, some large areas stand out for being remaining fragments of Atlantic Forest and degraded green areas, such as the Jardim Botânico Benjamin Maranhão, the Jacarapé State Park, the mouth of the Rio Cuiá, the Campo dos Escoteiros, the Sítio da Graça and Fazenda Mumbaba, among others, most of them in the interior of the municipality of João Pessoa. However, the main remnant of this plant formation is the Jardim Botânico Benjamin Maranhão, popularly known as Mata do Buraquinho.

Mata do Buraquinho occupies an area of ​​517 ha, corresponding to 2.5% of the area of ​​the municipality, spreading evenly within the city. However, at the beginning of the 20th century, this area reached 2500 ha, before being fragmented into smaller units, such as the Mangabeira Reserve, Mata da Penha, Mata do Altiplano and Mata de São Rafael (where UFPB was installed later) . In 1932, the architect from Rio de Janeiro Nestor Figueiredo executed an urban plan in the city, maintaining the forest, as he recognized its great environmental value (ALVES et al., 2009).

It corresponds to a leisure area, where hiking and a bit of local fauna and flora are known. It is a place where water resources combine with the biotic environment with great harmony, also conferring a historical and ecological value incorporated by the population. For a long time, it was in Mata do Buraquinho that the main water supply source of the municipality was located, which can be seen by the large number of artesian wells.

The Jaguaribe River crosses the site, with about 80% of the forest located on its right bank. In addition to Atlantic Forest , classified as an Open Ombrophilous Forest, it appears in the cerrado vegetation area, in the southeastern portion and ferns, where the humidity is higher, as in the area of ​​dams and in the flood terraces. The presence of the species ariticum-do-brejo, according to João Pessoa (2010) 1 , is a paleoenvironmental indicator of a fluviomarine environment in a recent geological past, which assumes that the marine influence reached a relatively large distance from the coast.

Mata de Restinga > (PONTES and BARBOSA, 2008). One of the last remnants of this formation, in Paraíba, is the Restinga de Cabedelo National Forest, popularly known as Mata da Amém, with approximately 103 ha, delimited by Decree SN of June 2, 2004, located in the municipality of Cabedelo. The Chico Mendes Institute for Biodiversity Conservation (ICMbio) is the body responsible for the administration of the Park, under the regional coordination (CR) of CR-6, in Cabedelo, and public visitation is only allowed under certain conditions established by ICMbio. Corresponds to an ecosystem of ecological and economic relevance, in which Rocha (1996) identified three main types of phytophysiognomies in the sandbank that extends from the northern part of João Pessoa to Cabedelo: sandbank fields, shrub-tree restinga, restinga forest (predominant ) and some areas altered by anthropic action, locally called “capoeiras” where grasses and small vegetation predominate. According to Carvalho and Carvalho (1985), it corresponds to a subcaducifolia, sclerophic vegetation, whose trees have stems and medium sizes, with 10 to 15 m in height, wide and irregular crowns and small leaves. The soils are rich in organic matter, favoring the existence of a shrub-tree vegetation (ARAUJO, 1993), but the porosity restricts humidity, hence the limited presence of epiphytes (CARVALHO and CARVALHO, 1985). Among the species characteristic of this forest, we can mention amescla ( Protium sp ), cupiúba ( Tapirira guianense ) and embaúba ( Cecropia sp >). In restinga fields, fruit species predominate, such as cashew ( Anacardium occidentale L. ) and mangabeira ( Hancornia speciosa ), according to Carvalho and Carvalho (1985). The Mandacaru River crosses the forest before it flows into the Paraíba River and, in this portion, an extensive area of ​​mangroves is present.

Thus, practically all the plains formed in the estuaries of the rivers in the area appear the vegetation of mangroves . The most imposing, however, is that of the Paraíba River estuary. This plant formation, relatively homogeneous, arboreal, halophilic and uniform, is, therefore, associated with indiscriminate mangrove soils, rich in decomposing organic matter, constituted by vegetal debris (leaves, roots and stems) and products of animal origin, with small granulometry and high salinity content, associated with the ebb and flow of the tides. On the south coast there is a gradation for shrub species as it enters the continent, as a consequence of the decrease in the salinity content and the increase in organic matter. In this area, the most present species are the pine-of-the-beach ( Mariscus maritimus ) and the ginger grass ( Cymbopogon martinii ), with fruit trees such as the cashew tree ( Anacardium occidentale ) and the mangabeira tree ( Hancoria speciosa ). The mangrove species found in the Paraíba River estuary and in the other coastal estuaries and lagoons present in the study area are the red mangrove ( Rizophora mangle L. ), the siriúba mangrove ( Avicenia tomentosa ), the button mangrove ( Conocarpus erectus L. ) and the white mangrove ( Laguncularia racemosa ) (GUEDES, 2002).

This subchapter dealt with a presentation of the plant formations in the area, so important for the establishment of Portuguese at the beginning of the colonial process and well portrayed in the works of travelers and narrators of the early centuries, from the letter by Pero Vaz, to those who passed through the Captaincy of Parahyba and praised the qualities of their forests, be it Brazil wood, mangroves or other formations.
